Most products treat privacy as a promise: a policy page, a toggle, a pledge that the company will behave. Promises depend on the trustworthiness — and the solvency — of whoever makes them. PRV Wallet is built on a different foundation. Privacy as **mathematics**: properties that hold because of how the system is constructed, not because of how a company chooses to act.

## Non-custodial by default

The first principle is the oldest one in this industry and the most ignored: *your keys, your coins.* PRV Wallet is non-custodial by default. The group never takes possession of user funds, which means there is no honeypot to breach, no balance sheet to commingle, and no counterparty whose failure can take your assets down with it. Self-custody removes an entire category of risk — the category responsible for the largest failures the industry has seen — by removing the custodian.

## Keys are math, and they stay on your device

A wallet is, at bottom, key management. In PRV Wallet, keys are derived and encrypted **on-device**. The cryptographic material that controls your assets is generated and protected where you are, not on a server you have to trust. This is what makes "non-custodial" more than a marketing word: there is no privileged copy of your keys sitting somewhere else.

## Privacy where it counts

Not every transaction needs the same privacy, and absolutism helps no one. The design principle is **provable privacy where it counts** — using zero-knowledge techniques and confidential-transaction approaches so that a user can prove what needs proving without exposing everything else. The goal is a wallet where privacy is a mathematical property of specific operations, applied deliberately, rather than a blanket claim that cannot be verified.

## Making self-custody usable

Self-custody has historically failed on usability, not philosophy. Seed phrases get lost; a single mistake is unforgiving. The hard, worthwhile engineering is making self-custody *safe enough for normal people* — sane recovery, sensible defaults, and an assistant (PRV Copilot) that helps without ever holding the keys. The bar is not "usable by crypto natives." The bar is "usable by your family."

## Why privacy is infrastructure

As money becomes machine-readable and agents begin to act on our behalf, the wallet becomes the surface where the most sensitive thing about us — what we own and what we do with it — is exposed or protected. That makes privacy a piece of infrastructure, not a preference. It has to be engineered into the foundation, because you cannot add it convincingly later.

> Non-custodial by default, keys derived and encrypted on-device, zero-knowledge where it counts. Privacy has to be provable, not promised.
