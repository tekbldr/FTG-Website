Welcome to **The Stack** — FTG's series on building the infrastructure of the digital economy. In this first episode, we lay out the whole thesis: why markets, money, and intelligence are converging into one stack, and why we chose to build and operate it rather than simply fund it.

These are the show notes. If you only read one section, read the takeaways.

## In this episode

We start from a single observation — that money is becoming programmable and machine-readable at the same moment that software is becoming an agent that can act — and follow it to its conclusion: the interface between money and machine intelligence is becoming one of the most important pieces of infrastructure of the decade. Then we get concrete about what it takes to build it.

## What we cover

- **The two curves.** Tokenization of value and agentic intelligence, and why their meeting point is the thing to own.
- **Why the stack has to be owned, not just funded.** The value is in the seams — shared identity, memory, and settlement — which a passive investor across rival companies can never build.
- **Three positions, one system.** Markets (the venue), money (the wallet), intelligence (the agent), connected by one backbone.
- **The compounding loop.** How liquidity, the wallet, and the AI make each other more valuable, and why loops beat features.
- **Operator-investor, in practice.** What it actually means to ship an exchange, a wallet, and an AI arm — custody discipline, key security, model orchestration — instead of advising from a board seat.
- **Region-first, global standard.** Why the Gulf is a launchpad rather than a follower, and what that means for how we build.

## Takeaways

1. **The prize is the seams, not any single layer.** Owning markets *or* money *or* intelligence is a business; owning the connective tissue between them is a compounding one.
2. **Build-and-operate is an edge, not a preference.** In infrastructure, the teams that run the thing learn what is real faster and can originate the companies the thesis requires.
3. **Loops are the moat.** Features get copied in a quarter; a loop across three owned layers is brutally hard to replicate.

> We are not building features. We are assembling the operating stack for how value will move, be secured, and be acted upon.
