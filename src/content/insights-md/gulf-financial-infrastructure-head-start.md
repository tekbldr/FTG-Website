Ask where the next generation of financial and AI infrastructure is being *built* — not theorized, but funded, powered, and shipped — and an unusual amount of the answer points to the Gulf. This is not a story about a single announcement. It is about four things arriving at once: capital, compute, models, and rules. When they line up in one place, that place gets a head start.

This is FTG Research's read on why we build region-first — with the caveat we apply to ourselves throughout: much of the headline capacity below is **announced and forward-looking**, not deployed and audited. We have preserved those qualifiers deliberately.

## The capital is already here

The Gulf's sovereign investors have moved from oil exporters to some of the most active technology allocators on earth.

- Saudi Arabia's **Public Investment Fund (PIF)** reported assets under management of roughly **$913 billion at year-end 2024**, up about 19% year on year, and in April 2025 **raised its 2030 AUM target to $2.67 trillion** — up nearly 43% from a prior $1.87 trillion goal. PIF has grown roughly 390% since 2016.
- Abu Dhabi's **Mubadala** deployed a record **$32.7 billion across around 40 transactions in 10 countries in 2025** (up ~12% year on year), which Global SWF ranked as the most active sovereign investor by number of deals that year.
- Globally, sovereign wealth funds **passed $15 trillion in assets for the first time in December 2025** (Global SWF).

**Why it matters:** infrastructure needs patient capital measured in decades, not exit windows. The Gulf has an unusual concentration of exactly that — and it is increasingly pointed at technology.

## The compute is being poured

Capital is turning into physical AI capacity at a scale that is rare outside the United States.

- In May 2025, PIF launched **HUMAIN**, a full-AI-value-chain subsidiary, which announced a partnership with **NVIDIA** to build AI infrastructure in Saudi Arabia — projected at **up to 500 megawatts of compute over five years**, scaling to several hundred thousand GPUs, with a first phase built around an 18,000-GPU GB300 supercomputer.
- In the UAE, partners including **G42, OpenAI, Oracle, NVIDIA, SoftBank, and Cisco** announced **Stargate UAE**, a 1-gigawatt cluster inside a new **5-gigawatt UAE–U.S. AI Campus** in Abu Dhabi, with the first 200-megawatt cluster **expected to go live in 2026**.

**Why it matters:** the AI economy runs on power and silicon. Whoever hosts the compute hosts a growing share of the intelligence that runs on top of it. (A fair caveat, which critics raise: "sovereign" compute still depends heavily on imported GPUs and export policy — so we treat the word as an ambition, not a settled fact.)

## The models speak the language

Compute without models is a warehouse of heaters. The region is shipping its own frontier-scale Arabic models.

- **Jais 2**, a 70-billion-parameter Arabic model pretrained from scratch on roughly 2.6 trillion tokens, was released in December 2025 by Inception (a G42 company), Cerebras, and MBZUAI's Institute of Foundation Models.
- **Falcon-H1 Arabic**, from Abu Dhabi's TII, launched in January 2026 on a hybrid Mamba-Transformer architecture in 3B/7B/34B sizes with context up to 256K tokens.
- Saudi Arabia's **ALLaM** (SDAIA) rounds out a genuinely competitive field.

We deliberately avoid ranking these — the "leading Arabic model" superlatives are vendor and single-leaderboard claims, contested across benchmarks. The signal that matters is not who is #1; it is that **the region now produces frontier Arabic models, not translations of English ones.**

## The rules are unusually clear

The Gulf did something most jurisdictions have not: it wrote digital-asset and payment rules early and explicitly. Dubai stood up a dedicated virtual-asset regulator (VARA); Abu Dhabi's ADGM/FSRA built a respected framework; the UAE's central bank introduced a payment-token (stablecoin) regime; and Saudi Arabia's central bank (SAMA) has advanced its own agenda. We are intentionally high-level here — specifics change quarter to quarter — but the direction is consistent: **clarity ahead of activity, rather than enforcement after the fact.**

**Why it matters:** regulatory clarity is not red tape; for infrastructure builders it is a *feature*, because it tells you what you are allowed to build before you build it.

## Why this is a head start, not a moment

Any one of these — capital, compute, models, rules — is a story. Together they are a *system*: patient money funding domestic compute, running domestic models, under domestic rules, for a young and overwhelmingly mobile-first population. That is the environment we chose to build in, region-first and to a global standard.

We are not claiming the Gulf has already won, and we are not part of the specific programs above. We are saying the ingredients for the next financial-and-intelligence infrastructure are unusually concentrated here — and that is exactly where an operator-investor wants to be building.
